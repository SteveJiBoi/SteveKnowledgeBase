Properties of Conductors in Electrostatic Equilibrium

Lecture 0471-1

In a conductor, electrons move freely — but once everything settles into electrostatic equilibrium (no moving charges), the conductor obeys a short list of powerful rules.

The Three Core Properties
  1. The field inside is zero. \(E_{\text{inside}}=0\). If it weren't, free charges would feel a force and move — contradicting equilibrium. A consequence is electrostatic shielding: anything inside a conductor is shielded from external fields.
  2. Excess charge lives on the surface. A Gaussian surface drawn just inside the conductor encloses zero field, so it encloses zero net charge — all excess charge must be on the outer surface.
  3. The field just outside is perpendicular to the surface and has magnitude \(E=\dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\). Any parallel component would push surface charges around, breaking equilibrium. Since \(\vec E\perp\) surface, the surface is an equipotential.
Field at a conductor's surface
\[ E_{\text{inside}}=0,\qquad E_{\text{surface}}=\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}\ \ (\perp\text{ to surface}) \]
Worked Example
Example Field at a charged surface

A conductor has local surface charge density \(\sigma=5.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{C/m}^2\). Find the field just outside that point.

\[ E=\frac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}=\frac{5.0\times10^{-6}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}\approx 5.6\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C, perpendicular to the surface.} \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. The electric field inside a solid conductor in electrostatic equilibrium is:
  • Azero everywhere
  • B\(\sigma/\varepsilon_0\)
  • C\(kQ/r^2\)
  • Duniform and nonzero
Answer A

If the interior field were nonzero, the free charges would accelerate, violating equilibrium. So \(E_{\text{inside}}=0\) — the basis of electrostatic shielding.

MCQ
Q2. Excess charge placed on a solid conductor in equilibrium resides:
  • Auniformly throughout the volume
  • Bat the center
  • Centirely on the outer surface
  • Donly on the bottom
Answer C

A Gaussian surface just inside the conductor has \(E=0\) on it, so it encloses zero net charge. All excess charge must therefore lie on the surface.

MCQ
Q3. The electric field just outside the surface of a charged conductor is:
  • Aparallel to the surface, \(E=\sigma/2\varepsilon_0\)
  • Bperpendicular to the surface, \(E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0\)
  • Czero
  • Dat \(45^\circ\) to the surface
Answer B

A parallel component would move surface charges, so the field must be perpendicular. A Gaussian pillbox gives \(E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0\) (note: twice the \(\sigma/2\varepsilon_0\) of an isolated sheet, because field exists on only one side).

FRQ
Q4. At a point on a charged conductor, the surface charge density is \(\sigma=8.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{C/m}^2\). (a) Find the field just outside that point. (b) State its direction and explain why.
Solution \(E\approx 9.0\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}\), perpendicular to the surface

(a) \(E=\dfrac{\sigma}{\varepsilon_0}=\dfrac{8.0\times10^{-6}}{8.85\times10^{-12}}\approx 9.0\times10^{5}\ \text{N/C}.\)

(b) Perpendicular (normal) to the surface. Any tangential component would exert a force along the surface on the free charges, causing them to move — which cannot happen in electrostatic equilibrium.

Irregularly Shaped Conductors

Lecture 0471-2

The fourth property of conductors: on a bumpy conductor, charge piles up where the surface curves most sharply — which is why lightning rods are pointed.

Core Concept
The fourth propertyFor an irregular shape, the local surface charge density is greatest where the radius of curvature is smallest — i.e., the most charge-per-area collects at the sharpest points. The field just outside (\(E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0\)) is therefore also strongest there.

To see why, model the conductor as two spheres of radii \(r_1>r_2\) joined by a long wire (so they share one potential, with \(d\gg r_1,r_2\)). When two conductors touch, charge redistributes until both are at the same potential.

Equal potential means \(\dfrac{kQ_1}{r_1}=\dfrac{kQ_2}{r_2}\), so \(Q\propto r\). But surface charge density \(\sigma=\dfrac{Q}{4\pi r^2}\propto\dfrac{r}{r^2}=\dfrac{1}{r}\). The smaller sphere (smaller \(r\)) ends up with the larger \(\sigma\) and the larger surface field.

Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. On an irregularly shaped charged conductor, the surface charge density is largest where the radius of curvature is:
  • Alargest (flattest region)
  • Bsmallest (sharpest point)
  • Cit is the same everywhere
  • Dexactly in the middle
Answer B

Since \(\sigma\propto 1/r\), the sharpest regions (smallest radius of curvature) accumulate the most charge per area — and the strongest field.

MCQ
Q2. When two conductors are connected by a wire and reach electrostatic equilibrium, they share the same:
  • Acharge
  • Bsurface charge density
  • Celectric potential
  • Dradius
Answer C

Connected conductors form one conductor, which is a single equipotential. They reach a common potential, not equal charge or charge density.

FRQ
Q3. Two conducting spheres of radii \(r_1\) and \(r_2\) (with \(r_1>r_2\)) are joined by a long wire and given charge. Show that the smaller sphere has the larger surface charge density, and relate this to why lightning rods are pointed.
Solution \(\sigma\propto 1/r\)

Connected ⇒ same potential: \(\dfrac{kQ_1}{r_1}=\dfrac{kQ_2}{r_2}\Rightarrow Q\propto r\). Then \(\sigma=\dfrac{Q}{4\pi r^2}\propto\dfrac{r}{r^2}=\dfrac1r\). The smaller \(r_2\) gives the larger \(\sigma\), and since \(E=\sigma/\varepsilon_0\), the strongest field is at the sharpest point. A pointed lightning rod concentrates charge and field at its tip, ionizing the surrounding air and channeling the strike.

Capacitance

Lecture 0472-1

A capacitor stores electric potential energy in an electric field. Capacitance measures how much charge it holds per volt.

Core Concepts

Connect a battery to two plates; charge flows until one plate holds \(+Q\) and the other \(-Q\). Capacitance is defined as the charge on the positive plate divided by the potential difference between the plates:

Definition of capacitance
\[ C\equiv\frac{Q}{\Delta V} \]
  • \(C\) = capacitance (farads, \(1\ \text{F}=1\ \text{C/V}\)); always positive
  • \(Q\) = magnitude of charge on one plate; the net charge of the capacitor is zero
  • \(\Delta V\) = potential difference between the plates
Capacitance is geometry, not chargeThe "\(\equiv\)" means defined as — not derived. Although \(C=Q/\Delta V\), the value of \(C\) depends only on the capacitor's physical properties (shape, size, and the material between the plates), not on how much charge you put on it. A capacitor has a capacitance even when uncharged.
Worked Example
Example Charge, voltage, capacitance

A capacitor holds \(12\ \mu\text{C}\) on its positive plate when connected across \(6.0\ \text{V}\). Find its capacitance.

\[ C=\frac{Q}{\Delta V}=\frac{12\times10^{-6}}{6.0}=2.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{F}=2.0\ \mu\text{F} \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. The net charge stored on a capacitor (both plates together) is:
  • Azero
  • B\(2Q\)
  • C\(Q\)
  • D\(C\,\Delta V^2\)
Answer A

One plate is \(+Q\), the other \(-Q\), so the total is zero. \(Q\) in \(C=Q/\Delta V\) refers to the magnitude on one plate.

MCQ
Q2. If you double the charge on a given capacitor, its capacitance:
  • Adoubles
  • Bhalves
  • Cstays the same
  • Dquadruples
Answer C

Capacitance depends only on geometry and materials. Doubling \(Q\) also doubles \(\Delta V\), leaving \(C=Q/\Delta V\) unchanged.

FRQ
Q3. A \(4.0\ \mu\text{F}\) capacitor is connected across a \(25\ \text{V}\) source. (a) Find the charge stored. (b) If the voltage is then doubled, what is the new charge?
Solution (a) \(100\ \mu\text{C}\); (b) \(200\ \mu\text{C}\)

(a) \(Q=C\,\Delta V=(4.0\times10^{-6})(25)=1.0\times10^{-4}\ \text{C}=100\ \mu\text{C}.\)

(b) \(C\) is fixed, so \(Q\propto\Delta V\): doubling the voltage doubles the charge to \(200\ \mu\text{C}.\)

Parallel-Plate Capacitor

Lectures 0472-2, 0701

The simplest capacitor: two parallel plates. Its capacitance comes straight from geometry.

Core Concepts

For an ideal parallel-plate capacitor (plates treated as infinite, uniform field between them), combining \(C=Q/\Delta V\) with the plate field gives:

Parallel-plate capacitance (vacuum)
\[ C=\frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d} \]
  • \(\varepsilon_0=8.85\times10^{-12}\ \text{F/m}\) = vacuum permittivity
  • \(A\) = area of one plate; \(d\) = separation between the plates
  • Larger plates or smaller gaps ⇒ more capacitance
AP-required shapesThe College Board expects you to be able to find the capacitance of three shapes: parallel-plate, spherical, and cylindrical capacitors (all from \(C=Q/\Delta V\) with Gauss's law for \(E\), then integrating for \(\Delta V\)).
Worked Example
Example Capacitance from dimensions

Two plates of area \(A=0.020\ \text{m}^2\) are separated by \(d=0.50\ \text{mm}\) of vacuum. Find \(C\).

\[ C=\frac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}=\frac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.020)}{0.50\times10^{-3}}\approx 3.5\times10^{-10}\ \text{F}=0.35\ \text{nF} \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. For a parallel-plate capacitor, if the plate separation \(d\) is doubled (everything else fixed), the capacitance:
  • Adoubles
  • Bis halved
  • Cis unchanged
  • Dquadruples
Answer B

\(C=\varepsilon_0 A/d\), so \(C\propto 1/d\). Doubling \(d\) halves \(C\).

MCQ
Q2. Which change increases the capacitance of a parallel-plate capacitor?
  • Aincreasing the plate area
  • Bincreasing the plate separation
  • Cdecreasing the charge
  • Ddecreasing the applied voltage
Answer A

\(C=\varepsilon_0 A/d\) depends only on geometry. Larger \(A\) raises \(C\); \(Q\) and \(\Delta V\) do not affect \(C\).

FRQ
Q3. A vacuum parallel-plate capacitor has plates of area \(0.040\ \text{m}^2\) separated by \(2.0\ \text{mm}\). (a) Find its capacitance. (b) Find the charge stored when connected to \(12\ \text{V}\).
Solution (a) \(\approx 0.18\ \text{nF}\); (b) \(\approx 2.1\ \text{nC}\)

(a) \(C=\dfrac{\varepsilon_0 A}{d}=\dfrac{(8.85\times10^{-12})(0.040)}{2.0\times10^{-3}}\approx 1.8\times10^{-10}\ \text{F}.\)

(b) \(Q=C\,\Delta V=(1.8\times10^{-10})(12)\approx 2.1\times10^{-9}\ \text{C}=2.1\ \text{nC}.\)

Dielectrics & Permittivity

Lectures 0701, 0702

Slip an insulator between the plates and the capacitance goes up. The dielectric constant \(\kappa\) captures how much.

Core Concepts

A dielectric is an insulating material placed between the plates. Its molecules polarize, creating an induced field opposite the capacitor's field, which reduces the net field. The dielectric constant \(\kappa\) (lowercase kappa) scales the capacitance:

Capacitance with a dielectric
\[ C=\frac{\kappa\varepsilon_0 A}{d},\qquad \kappa=\frac{\varepsilon}{\varepsilon_0}\ \ (\geq 1,\ \text{no units}) \]
Isolated capacitor + dielectric (Q fixed)If the capacitor is disconnected (charge can't change) and you insert a dielectric: \(Q\) and \(d\) stay the same, the field \(E\) decreases, so \(\Delta V=Ed\) decreases, and since \(C=Q/\Delta V\), the capacitance increases (by the factor \(\kappa\)). Vacuum has \(\kappa=1\) exactly; air is \(\approx1.0006\); every real dielectric has \(\kappa>1\).
Worked Example
Example Adding a dielectric

A vacuum capacitor has \(C_0=0.40\ \mu\text{F}\). A dielectric with \(\kappa=3.0\) completely fills the gap. Find the new capacitance.

\[ C=\kappa C_0=(3.0)(0.40\ \mu\text{F})=1.2\ \mu\text{F} \]

If the capacitor were isolated with charge \(Q\), the voltage would drop to \(\Delta V_0/\kappa\) — one-third of its original value.

Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. Inserting a dielectric into an electrically isolated charged capacitor causes the capacitance to:
  • Aincrease
  • Bdecrease
  • Cstay the same
  • Ddrop to zero
Answer A

The dielectric reduces \(E\) and hence \(\Delta V\); with \(Q\) fixed, \(C=Q/\Delta V\) increases by a factor of \(\kappa\).

MCQ
Q2. The dielectric constant \(\kappa\) of any real dielectric material is:
  • Aexactly 1
  • Bless than 1
  • Cgreater than 1
  • Dnegative
Answer C

\(\kappa=\varepsilon/\varepsilon_0\geq 1\). Only a vacuum has \(\kappa=1\) exactly; all real dielectrics polarize, giving \(\kappa>1\).

MCQ
Q3. Why must a dielectric be an insulator rather than a conductor?
  • AConductors have \(\kappa<1\).
  • BA conductor would let charge flow between the plates, short-circuiting the capacitor.
  • CConductors cannot polarize at all.
  • DInsulators decrease capacitance.
Answer B

If a conductor bridged the plates, charge would flow across and the capacitor would short out. A dielectric polarizes but does not conduct.

FRQ
Q4. A parallel-plate capacitor has capacitance \(C_0=0.50\ \mu\text{F}\) with vacuum between the plates. A dielectric of constant \(\kappa=4.0\) is inserted to fill the gap. (a) Find the new capacitance. (b) If the capacitor was isolated holding charge \(Q\), by what factor does the voltage across it change?
Solution (a) \(2.0\ \mu\text{F}\); (b) voltage drops to \(\tfrac14\)

(a) \(C=\kappa C_0=(4.0)(0.50\ \mu\text{F})=2.0\ \mu\text{F}.\)

(b) With \(Q\) fixed, \(\Delta V=Q/C\). Since \(C\) grew by \(4\times\), \(\Delta V\) falls to \(\tfrac14\) of its original value.

Energy Stored in a Capacitor

Lecture 0472-3

Charging a capacitor takes work — that work is stored in the electric field, ready to be released (think camera flash).

Core Concept

Move charge across the plates one \(dq\) at a time. The first bit is free (\(\Delta V=0\)), but each later bit costs \(dW=\Delta V\,dq=\dfrac{q}{C}\,dq\). Integrating from \(0\) to \(Q\) gives the stored energy, expressible three equivalent ways:

Energy stored in a capacitor
\[ U_C=\tfrac12 Q\,\Delta V=\tfrac12 C\,\Delta V^2=\frac{Q^2}{2C} \]
Which form to use?Pick the version with the two quantities you know. Use \(\tfrac12 C\Delta V^2\) when the capacitor stays connected to a fixed voltage; use \(Q^2/2C\) when it's isolated with fixed charge. The energy is stored in the electric field between the plates.
Worked Example
Example Energy at a given voltage

A \(2.0\ \mu\text{F}\) capacitor is charged to \(100\ \text{V}\). How much energy is stored?

\[ U_C=\tfrac12 C\,\Delta V^2=\tfrac12(2.0\times10^{-6})(100)^2=0.010\ \text{J}=10\ \text{mJ} \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. A capacitor is connected to a fixed voltage. If that voltage is doubled, the stored energy:
  • Astays the same
  • Bdoubles
  • Cis halved
  • Dquadruples
Answer D

\(U=\tfrac12 C\,\Delta V^2\), so \(U\propto\Delta V^2\). Doubling the voltage multiplies the energy by \(4\).

MCQ
Q2. Which expression is not a correct form for the energy stored in a capacitor?
  • A\(\tfrac12 C\,\Delta V^2\)
  • B\(\tfrac12 Q\,\Delta V\)
  • C\(\dfrac{Q^2}{2C}\)
  • D\(\dfrac{C^2}{2Q}\)
Answer D

The three valid forms are \(\tfrac12 Q\Delta V\), \(\tfrac12 C\Delta V^2\), and \(Q^2/2C\) (all equal using \(Q=C\Delta V\)). \(C^2/2Q\) is not among them.

FRQ
Q3. A \(50\ \mu\text{F}\) capacitor (like one in a camera flash) is charged to \(200\ \text{V}\). Find (a) the energy stored and (b) the charge on a plate.
Solution (a) \(1.0\ \text{J}\); (b) \(10\ \text{mC}\)

(a) \(U=\tfrac12 C\,\Delta V^2=\tfrac12(50\times10^{-6})(200)^2=\tfrac12(50\times10^{-6})(4.0\times10^4)=1.0\ \text{J}.\)

(b) \(Q=C\,\Delta V=(50\times10^{-6})(200)=1.0\times10^{-2}\ \text{C}=10\ \text{mC}.\)

Electric Current

Lectures 0473-1, Ch.19

Current is simply moving charge — the rate at which charge passes a point in a wire.

Core Concepts
Definition of current
\[ I=\frac{dQ}{dt}\qquad\left(\text{average: } I_{\text{avg}}=\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}\right) \]
  • \(I\) = current, in amperes (\(1\ \text{A}=1\ \text{C/s}\)); the ampere is an SI base unit
  • Current flows only when there is a potential difference across the wire
Conventional currentBy convention, current points in the direction positive charges would flow. In most wires the actual movers are electrons, drifting the opposite way. Unless told otherwise, use conventional current.
Worked Example (from your notes)
Example Current and electron count

A charge of \(13.0\ \text{mC}\) passes a cross-section of wire in \(4.5\ \text{s}\). Find (a) the current and (b) the number of electrons.

\[ \text{(a)}\quad I=\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t}=\frac{0.013}{4.5}\approx 2.9\times10^{-3}\ \text{A}=2.9\ \text{mA} \]
\[ \text{(b)}\quad n=\frac{Q}{e}=\frac{0.013}{1.60\times10^{-19}}\approx 8.1\times10^{16}\ \text{electrons} \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. One ampere is equivalent to:
  • Aone volt per ohm-second
  • Bone coulomb per second
  • Cone joule per coulomb
  • Done watt per volt-second
Answer B

\(I=dQ/dt\), so \(1\ \text{A}=1\ \text{C/s}\).

MCQ
Q2. Conventional current is defined as the direction in which:
  • Apositive charge would flow
  • Belectrons actually flow
  • Cthe wire points
  • Dthe magnetic field points
Answer A

Conventional current follows positive-charge flow, which is opposite to the electrons' actual drift in a metal wire.

MCQ
Q3. A charge of \(6.0\ \text{C}\) passes a point in \(2.0\ \text{s}\). The current is:
  • A\(12\ \text{A}\)
  • B\(0.33\ \text{A}\)
  • C\(3.0\ \text{A}\)
  • D\(8.0\ \text{A}\)
Answer C

\(I=\Delta Q/\Delta t=6.0/2.0=3.0\ \text{A}.\)

FRQ
Q4. A steady current of \(0.50\ \text{A}\) flows in a wire for \(1.0\ \text{minute}\). (a) How much charge passes a cross-section? (b) How many electrons is that?
Solution (a) \(30\ \text{C}\); (b) \(\approx 1.9\times10^{20}\) electrons

(a) \(\Delta Q=I\,\Delta t=(0.50)(60)=30\ \text{C}.\)

(b) \(n=\dfrac{Q}{e}=\dfrac{30}{1.60\times10^{-19}}\approx 1.9\times10^{20}\) electrons.

Drift Velocity

Lecture 0473-1

Current links to the microscopic motion of charge carriers through the wire — a surprisingly slow drift.

Core Concept
Current in terms of drift velocity
\[ I=nqv_d A \]
  • \(n\) = charge-carrier density (carriers per m³)
  • \(q\) = charge per carrier; \(A\) = cross-sectional area of the wire
  • \(v_d\) = drift velocity, the average velocity of the carriers
Why lights turn on instantlyDrift velocity is tiny — on the order of \(0.1\ \text{mm/s}\). Yet a lamp lights immediately when you flip the switch because the wire is already full of electrons everywhere; they all begin moving at once, like water already filling a hose.
Worked Example
Example Drift speed in a copper wire

A copper wire (\(n=8.5\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}\)) of cross-section \(A=1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2\) carries \(I=1.0\ \text{A}\). Find the drift velocity.

\[ v_d=\frac{I}{nqA}=\frac{1.0}{(8.5\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(1.0\times10^{-6})}\approx 7.4\times10^{-5}\ \text{m/s} \]

That's about \(0.07\ \text{mm/s}\) — slower than a snail, confirming how sluggish the drift really is.

Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. In \(I=nqv_dA\), if the wire's cross-sectional area is halved while the current is held constant, the drift velocity:
  • Ais halved
  • Bis unchanged
  • Cdoubles
  • Dquadruples
Answer C

\(v_d=\dfrac{I}{nqA}\propto\dfrac1A\). Halving \(A\) (with \(I\) fixed) doubles the drift velocity.

MCQ
Q2. A lamp lights almost instantly when the switch closes, even though drift velocity is only ~0.1 mm/s, because:
  • Aelectrons travel at the speed of light
  • Bthe wire is already full of electrons that all start moving together
  • Cthe filament creates new electrons
  • Ddrift velocity is actually very large
Answer B

The carriers are present throughout the wire and begin drifting essentially simultaneously when the field is established, so current flows everywhere almost at once.

FRQ
Q3. A wire with carrier density \(n=5.0\times10^{28}\ \text{m}^{-3}\) and cross-section \(A=2.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2\) carries a current of \(3.0\ \text{A}\). Find the drift velocity of the carriers (\(q=1.6\times10^{-19}\ \text{C}\)).
Solution \(v_d\approx 1.9\times10^{-4}\ \text{m/s}\)
\[ v_d=\frac{I}{nqA}=\frac{3.0}{(5.0\times10^{28})(1.6\times10^{-19})(2.0\times10^{-6})}\approx 1.9\times10^{-4}\ \text{m/s} \]

Resistance & Ohm's Law

Chapter 19

Resistance measures how strongly an object opposes current. For ohmic materials, current and voltage are simply proportional.

Core Concepts
Ohm's Law
\[ R=\frac{\Delta V}{I}\qquad\Longleftrightarrow\qquad \Delta V=IR \]
  • \(R\) = resistance, in ohms (\(1\ \Omega=1\ \text{V/A}\)) — a property of the object
  • Ohmic materials obey \(\Delta V=IR\) with constant \(R\) (a straight \(I\)–\(V\) line); non-ohmic ones do not
  • We treat connecting wires as having zero resistance unless told otherwise
Worked Example
Example Resistance from voltage and current

A resistor carries \(3.0\ \text{A}\) when \(12\ \text{V}\) is applied across it. Find its resistance.

\[ R=\frac{\Delta V}{I}=\frac{12}{3.0}=4.0\ \Omega \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. An ohmic resistor is one for which:
  • Aresistance increases with current
  • B\(\Delta V\) and \(I\) are proportional (constant \(R\))
  • Ccurrent flows with no voltage
  • Dresistance is always zero
Answer B

Ohmic materials obey \(\Delta V=IR\) with constant \(R\), giving a straight line through the origin on an \(I\)–\(V\) graph.

MCQ
Q2. A \(3.0\ \Omega\) resistor has \(9.0\ \text{V}\) across it. The current through it is:
  • A\(27\ \text{A}\)
  • B\(0.33\ \text{A}\)
  • C\(3.0\ \text{A}\)
  • D\(6.0\ \text{A}\)
Answer C

\(I=\dfrac{\Delta V}{R}=\dfrac{9.0}{3.0}=3.0\ \text{A}.\)

FRQ
Q3. A device draws \(0.40\ \text{A}\) at \(6.0\ \text{V}\) and \(0.80\ \text{A}\) at \(12\ \text{V}\). (a) Find the resistance at each voltage. (b) Is the device ohmic? Explain.
Solution \(R=15\ \Omega\) both times; yes, ohmic

(a) \(R_1=\dfrac{6.0}{0.40}=15\ \Omega\); \(R_2=\dfrac{12}{0.80}=15\ \Omega.\)

(b) The resistance is the same at both voltages (constant \(R\), proportional \(I\) and \(\Delta V\)), so the device is ohmic.

Resistivity

Lecture 0345

Resistance describes an object; resistivity describes the material it's made of.

Core Concepts

Resistance \(R\) is a property of an object; resistivity \(\rho\) is a fundamental property of a material. They're connected by the object's dimensions:

Resistance from resistivity
\[ R=\frac{\rho L}{A} \]
  • \(\rho\) = resistivity (units \(\Omega\cdot\text{m}\)) — material property
  • \(L\) = length; \(A\) = cross-sectional area of the object
  • A long, thin wire has more resistance than a short, fat one of the same material
Temperature dependenceResistivity changes with temperature: for conductors, \(\rho\) increases as temperature rises; for semiconductors, \(\rho\) decreases as temperature rises. (Typical 20°C values: copper \(1.7\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}\), silver \(1.6\times10^{-8}\), glass \(\sim10^{11}\).)
Worked Example
Example Resistance of a copper wire

Find the resistance of a copper wire (\(\rho=1.7\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}\)) of length \(2.0\ \text{m}\) and cross-sectional area \(1.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2\).

\[ R=\frac{\rho L}{A}=\frac{(1.7\times10^{-8})(2.0)}{1.0\times10^{-6}}=3.4\times10^{-2}\ \Omega=0.034\ \Omega \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. A wire is stretched/replaced so its length doubles while its cross-sectional area stays the same. Its resistance:
  • Ais halved
  • Bis unchanged
  • Cdoubles
  • Dquarters
Answer C

\(R=\rho L/A\propto L\). Doubling the length doubles the resistance.

MCQ
Q2. Which statement correctly distinguishes the two quantities?
  • AResistance is a property of the object; resistivity is a property of the material.
  • BResistance is a material property; resistivity is an object property.
  • CThey are identical.
  • DBoth depend only on temperature.
Answer A

\(\rho\) is intrinsic to the material; \(R=\rho L/A\) also depends on the object's length and area.

MCQ
Q3. As a copper conductor is heated, its resistivity:
  • Aincreases
  • Bdecreases
  • Cstays exactly constant
  • Ddrops to zero
Answer A

For conductors, resistivity rises with temperature (more lattice vibration scatters the carriers). Semiconductors behave oppositely.

FRQ
Q4. An aluminum wire (\(\rho=2.8\times10^{-8}\ \Omega\cdot\text{m}\)) is \(5.0\ \text{m}\) long with a cross-sectional area of \(2.0\times10^{-6}\ \text{m}^2\). Find its resistance.
Solution \(R\approx 0.070\ \Omega\)
\[ R=\frac{\rho L}{A}=\frac{(2.8\times10^{-8})(5.0)}{2.0\times10^{-6}}=7.0\times10^{-2}\ \Omega \]

Graphing Resistivity

Lecture 0346

A classic AP lab: design a graph whose slope is the resistivity of a wire.

Designing the Graph

Start from Ohm's law and the resistivity relation and set them equal:

\[ R=\frac{\Delta V}{I}=\frac{\rho L}{A}\ \Longrightarrow\ \Delta V=\rho\left(\frac{IL}{A}\right) \]

Compare to \(y=mx+b\): plot \(\Delta V\) on the \(y\)-axis and \(\dfrac{IL}{A}\) on the \(x\)-axis, and the slope is the resistivity \(\rho\).

Two subtle lab points(1) The \(y\)-intercept isn't quite zero — it's a small positive value because the connecting wires have a little resistance (the derivation assumed zero). (2) The applied voltage was reduced as the wire was shortened, to keep the nichrome from heating up — since a conductor's resistivity rises with temperature.
Worked Example — the wire's area
Example Cross-section of 32-gauge wire

A 32-gauge wire has diameter \(0.202\ \text{mm}\), so radius \(r=0.101\ \text{mm}=1.01\times10^{-4}\ \text{m}\). Its cross-sectional area is:

\[ A=\pi r^2=\pi(1.01\times10^{-4})^2\approx 3.2\times10^{-8}\ \text{m}^2 \]

This \(A\) is needed to compute the \(x\)-values \(\dfrac{IL}{A}\) for the graph.

Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. To make resistivity the slope of a best-fit line, you should plot \(\Delta V\) versus:
  • A\(I\)
  • B\(L\)
  • C\(\dfrac{IL}{A}\)
  • D\(\dfrac{A}{IL}\)
Answer C

From \(\Delta V=\rho\left(\dfrac{IL}{A}\right)\), plotting \(\Delta V\) vs \(\dfrac{IL}{A}\) gives a line of slope \(\rho\).

MCQ
Q2. In this experiment, the best-fit line has a small positive \(y\)-intercept rather than passing through the origin. The best explanation is:
  • Athe nichrome has negative resistance
  • Bthe connecting wires have a small resistance the model ignored
  • Cthe ammeter reads zero
  • Dresistivity is negative
Answer B

The derivation assumed zero-resistance leads. Real connecting wires add a small constant resistance, shifting the intercept slightly positive.

FRQ
Q3. Starting from Ohm's law and \(R=\rho L/A\), show how to design a graph whose slope is the resistivity of a wire, and identify the axes.
Solution

Set the two expressions for \(R\) equal: \(\dfrac{\Delta V}{I}=\dfrac{\rho L}{A}\). Clear the denominators to get \(\Delta V=\rho\left(\dfrac{IL}{A}\right)\). Comparing to \(y=mx+b\): let \(y=\Delta V\) and \(x=\dfrac{IL}{A}\). The line's slope is \(\rho\) (the intercept should be ~0). Measure \(\Delta V\) and \(I\) for several wire lengths \(L\), using \(A=\pi r^2\) for the wire, then plot and take the slope.

Electric Power

Chapter 19

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted to heat, light, and sound — and it comes in three handy forms.

Core Concepts

Starting from \(P=\dfrac{\Delta PE}{t}=\dfrac{q\,\Delta V}{t}=I\,\Delta V\) and combining with \(\Delta V=IR\) gives three equivalent expressions:

Electric power
\[ P=I\,\Delta V=I^2R=\frac{\Delta V^2}{R} \]
  • \(P\) = power, in watts (\(1\ \text{W}=1\ \text{J/s}\))
  • Use the form matching your known quantities; all three are equal via \(\Delta V=IR\)
Worked Example
Example A 60-watt bulb

A \(60\ \text{W}\) light bulb operates on a \(120\ \text{V}\) household circuit. Find (a) the current it draws and (b) its resistance.

\[ \text{(a)}\quad I=\frac{P}{\Delta V}=\frac{60}{120}=0.50\ \text{A} \]
\[ \text{(b)}\quad R=\frac{\Delta V^2}{P}=\frac{120^2}{60}=240\ \Omega \]
Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. Which expression is not equal to the power dissipated in a resistor?
  • A\(I\,\Delta V\)
  • B\(I^2R\)
  • C\(\dfrac{\Delta V^2}{R}\)
  • D\(\dfrac{R}{I^2}\)
Answer D

The three equivalent forms are \(I\Delta V\), \(I^2R\), and \(\Delta V^2/R\). \(R/I^2\) is not a power expression.

MCQ
Q2. A current of \(2.0\ \text{A}\) flows through a \(10\ \Omega\) resistor. The power dissipated is:
  • A\(5.0\ \text{W}\)
  • B\(20\ \text{W}\)
  • C\(40\ \text{W}\)
  • D\(400\ \text{W}\)
Answer C

\(P=I^2R=(2.0)^2(10)=40\ \text{W}.\)

FRQ
Q3. A \(100\ \text{W}\) light bulb runs on \(120\ \text{V}\). Find (a) the current it draws and (b) its operating resistance.
Solution (a) \(\approx 0.83\ \text{A}\); (b) \(144\ \Omega\)

(a) \(I=\dfrac{P}{\Delta V}=\dfrac{100}{120}\approx 0.83\ \text{A}.\)

(b) \(R=\dfrac{\Delta V^2}{P}=\dfrac{120^2}{100}=144\ \Omega.\)

Energy Cost & the Kilowatt-Hour

Chapter 19

Your electric bill is charged by energy, measured in kilowatt-hours — a unit you should be able to derive on demand.

Core Concepts

A kilowatt-hour is the energy used by a \(1\ \text{kW}\) device running for \(1\ \text{hour}\). Convert it to joules:

The kilowatt-hour
\[ 1\ \text{kW}\cdot\text{h}=(1000\ \text{W})(3600\ \text{s})=3.6\times10^{6}\ \text{J}=3.6\ \text{MJ} \]
\[ \text{Energy}=P\,t,\qquad \text{Cost}=(\text{energy in kWh})\times(\text{rate per kWh}) \]
Derive it every timeYou won't be given \(1\ \text{kWh}=3.6\ \text{MJ}\) on the exam — derive it from \((1000\ \text{W})(3600\ \text{s})\). Power × time = energy.
Worked Example (from your notes)
Example Resistance of an efficient bulb & cost

An efficient \(15\ \text{W}\) bulb runs on \(120\ \text{V}\). Its operating resistance:

\[ R=\frac{\Delta V^2}{P}=\frac{120^2}{15}=960\ \Omega \]

Now suppose three such bulbs replace three \(65\ \text{W}\) bulbs, saving \(3\times(65-15)=150\ \text{W}=0.15\ \text{kW}\), run \(3\ \text{h/day}\):

\[ \text{Energy saved/day}=(0.15\ \text{kW})(3\ \text{h})=0.45\ \text{kWh}\ \Rightarrow\ \text{cost saved}=0.45\times\$0.12\approx \$0.054/\text{day} \]

That's roughly \(\$20\) per year — so a \(\$108\) up-front cost pays back in about \(5.5\) years.

Exam-Style Practice
MCQ
Q1. The kilowatt-hour is a unit of:
  • Apower
  • Benergy
  • Ccurrent
  • Dcharge
Answer B

Power × time = energy. A kW (power) times an hour (time) is energy: \(1\ \text{kWh}=3.6\ \text{MJ}\).

MCQ
Q2. One kilowatt-hour equals how many joules?
  • A\(3600\ \text{J}\)
  • B\(1000\ \text{J}\)
  • C\(3.6\times10^{6}\ \text{J}\)
  • D\(1.0\times10^{6}\ \text{J}\)
Answer C

\(1\ \text{kWh}=(1000\ \text{W})(3600\ \text{s})=3.6\times10^{6}\ \text{J}=3.6\ \text{MJ}.\)

FRQ
Q3. A \(1500\ \text{W}\) space heater runs for \(4.0\ \text{hours}\). Electricity costs \(\$0.15\) per kWh. (a) How much energy does it use, in kWh and in joules? (b) What does it cost to run?
Solution (a) \(6.0\ \text{kWh}=2.16\times10^{7}\ \text{J}\); (b) \(\$0.90\)

(a) \(E=Pt=(1.5\ \text{kW})(4.0\ \text{h})=6.0\ \text{kWh}=6.0\times3.6\times10^6=2.16\times10^{7}\ \text{J}.\)

(b) Cost \(=6.0\ \text{kWh}\times\$0.15/\text{kWh}=\$0.90.\)
