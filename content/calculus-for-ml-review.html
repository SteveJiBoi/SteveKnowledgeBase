Week 1

Derivatives & Optimization

One variable · the slope of everything

A derivative measures how fast a function changes. In machine learning, the function is a cost (how wrong the model is), and we change the model to make that cost shrink. Finding where the derivative is zero is how we find the best model.

Why calculus? A machine-learning warm-up

Suppose you predict a house's price from its number of bedrooms. You collect data:

Bedrooms1235678910
Price$150k$250k$350k$600k$650k$750k$800k?$1,050k

A model is a rule, e.g. a straight line price \(= m\cdot(\text{bedrooms}) + b\). Different \(m,b\) fit the data better or worse. We score a model by its cost — typically the sum of squared errors between predictions and reality:

$$\text{Cost}(m,b)=\sum_{i}\big(\underbrace{m\,x_i+b}_{\text{prediction}}-\underbrace{y_i}_{\text{actual}}\big)^2$$
🤖 The big idea

Training a model = minimizing its cost. The cost is a function of the model's parameters, and the minimum is where its derivative is zero. That single sentence is the spine of this entire course — Week 1 builds derivatives, Week 2 extends them to many parameters, Week 3 sharpens the search.

What is a derivative?

The derivative of \(f\) at a point is the slope of the tangent line there — the function's instantaneous rate of change. We build it from the slope of a secant line through two nearby points and then slide them together.

$$f'(x)=\frac{df}{dx}=\lim_{\Delta x \to 0}\frac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}$$

The fraction is "rise over run": the change in height \(f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)\) divided by the change in input \(\Delta x\). As \(\Delta x \to 0\), the secant becomes the tangent.

🚗 The everyday picture: velocity

If \(x(t)\) is the position of a car at time \(t\), then average velocity over an interval is \(\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\) (distance ÷ time). The instantaneous velocity — what the speedometer reads — is the derivative \(x'(t)\): shrink the time interval to zero.

Worked exampleDerivative of \(f(x)=x^2\) from first principles
  1. Write the difference quotient.\(\dfrac{f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)}{\Delta x}=\dfrac{(x+\Delta x)^2-x^2}{\Delta x}\)
  2. Expand the numerator.\((x+\Delta x)^2-x^2 = x^2+2x\,\Delta x+\Delta x^2 - x^2 = 2x\,\Delta x+\Delta x^2\)
  3. Divide by \(\Delta x\).\(\dfrac{2x\,\Delta x+\Delta x^2}{\Delta x}=2x+\Delta x\)
  4. Let \(\Delta x\to 0\).The leftover \(\Delta x\) vanishes.
✓
\(f'(x)=2x\). The slope of \(x^2\) at \(x=3\) is \(6\); at the bottom \(x=0\) the slope is \(0\) — that flat point is exactly where a minimum lives.

⭐ The power rule

For any power, bring the exponent down front and subtract one from it: \(\frac{d}{dx}x^n = n\,x^{n-1}\). It covers \(x^2,\,x^3,\,\sqrt x,\,\tfrac1x\) and everything in between.

ExampleCubic from first principles
  1. Expand.\((x+\Delta x)^3 = x^3+3x^2\Delta x+3x\Delta x^2+\Delta x^3\)
  2. Subtract \(x^3\), divide by \(\Delta x\).\(3x^2+3x\,\Delta x+\Delta x^2\)
  3. \(\Delta x\to 0\).only \(3x^2\) survives
✓
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}x^3=3x^2\) — matches the power rule.
ExampleDerivative of \(\frac{1}{x}\)
  1. Rewrite as a power.\(\tfrac1x = x^{-1}\)
  2. Apply the power rule with \(n=-1\).\(-1\cdot x^{-2}\)
  3. Tidy up.\(-x^{-2}=-\dfrac{1}{x^2}\)
✓
Always negative — \(\tfrac1x\) always slopes downhill.

Combining functions: sum, scalar, product, chain

Sum rule

$$(f+g)' = f' + g'$$

Differentiate term by term.

Scalar multiple

$$(c\,f)' = c\,f'$$

Constants ride along untouched.

Product rule

$$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$$

"derivative of first × second, plus first × derivative of second."

Chain rule key for ML

$$\big(f(g(x))\big)' = f'(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$

Differentiate the outside, then multiply by the derivative of the inside. This rule is backpropagation.

Worked exampleChain rule: differentiate \(h(x)=(3x^2+1)^5\)
  1. Spot inside & outside.Inside \(g(x)=3x^2+1\); outside \(f(u)=u^5\).
  2. Differentiate the outside (keep inside).\(f'(u)=5u^4 \;\Rightarrow\; 5(3x^2+1)^4\)
  3. Multiply by the derivative of the inside.\(g'(x)=6x\)
  4. Combine.\(5(3x^2+1)^4\cdot 6x\)
✓
\(h'(x)=30x\,(3x^2+1)^4\).
Worked exampleProduct rule: differentiate \(p(x)=x^2 e^x\)
  1. Label the two factors.\(f=x^2\;(f'=2x)\), \(g=e^x\;(g'=e^x)\).
  2. Apply \((fg)'=f'g+fg'\).\(2x\,e^x + x^2 e^x\)
  3. Factor.\(e^x(2x + x^2)\)
✓
\(p'(x)=x e^x(x+2)\).

The star functions: \(e\), logarithms & inverses

Where \(e\) comes from — compound interest

Put $1 in the bank at 100% interest. Compound it more and more often and the year-end balance climbs toward a magic ceiling:

CompoundingFormulaYear-end $
Once a year\((1+1)^1\)2.000
Twice (every 6 mo)\((1+\tfrac12)^2\)2.250
3× (every 4 mo)\((1+\tfrac13)^3\)2.370
Monthly\((1+\tfrac1{12})^{12}\)2.613
\(n\to\infty\)\(\big(1+\tfrac1n\big)^{n}\)e ≈ 2.71828
$$e=\lim_{n\to\infty}\Big(1+\frac1n\Big)^{n}\approx 2.71828\qquad\text{and uniquely}\qquad \frac{d}{dx}e^{x}=e^{x}$$
⭐ Why \(e^x\) is special

It is the one function (up to scaling) that is its own derivative: its slope at every point equals its height. That self-similarity makes it the natural language of growth, probability, and the sigmoid/softmax activations you'll meet in Week 3.

Logarithm & the derivative of an inverse

\(\ln x\) is the inverse of \(e^x\). For any invertible \(f\) with inverse \(f^{-1}\), reflecting the graph across \(y=x\) reflects the slopes too, giving:

$$\big(f^{-1}\big)'(y)=\frac{1}{f'\!\big(f^{-1}(y)\big)}$$
Worked exampleUse the inverse rule to get \(\frac{d}{dx}\ln x\)
  1. Set up.Let \(f(x)=e^x\), so \(f^{-1}(y)=\ln y\) and \(f'(x)=e^x\).
  2. Apply the inverse rule.\((\ln)'(y)=\dfrac{1}{f'(\ln y)}=\dfrac{1}{e^{\ln y}}\)
  3. Simplify \(e^{\ln y}=y\).\(\dfrac1y\)
✓
\(\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x=\dfrac1x\).

When a derivative doesn't exist

A function is non-differentiable at a point if it has no single well-defined tangent there. Three culprits from the slides:

Three ways a derivative can fail to exist. ReLU, \(\max(0,x)\), is the corner case — ubiquitous in neural networks.
  • Corner / cusp — e.g. \(|x|\) at \(x=0\): the slope from the left (\(-1\)) ≠ the slope from the right (\(+1\)).
  • Jump / discontinuity — the function teleports; you can't even draw one tangent.
  • Vertical tangent — e.g. \(\sqrt[3]{x}\) at \(0\): the slope blows up to \(\infty\).
⚠️ Why ML cares

The popular ReLU activation \(\max(0,x)\) has a corner at \(0\) — exactly this situation. In practice we just pick a "subgradient" (say \(0\)) there and carry on.

Optimization: find where the slope is zero

At a smooth maximum or minimum the tangent is horizontal, so the recipe is: set \(f'(x)=0\) and solve. This is the moment calculus becomes machine learning.

At a smooth peak or valley the tangent is flat (\(f'=0\)). The derivative is positive going uphill, negative going downhill — and flips sign through each turning point.
🤖 Least squares & the "power-line" problem

Your slides minimise the sum of squared distances from a point to a set of houses (the "power-line" setup). The punchline is beautiful: the minimizer is the average. That is precisely why squared-error cost gives you the mean — and why linear regression works.

Worked exampleTwo power lines: minimize \(C(x)=(x-a)^2+(x-b)^2\)
  1. Differentiate (chain rule on each square).\(C'(x)=2(x-a)+2(x-b)\)
  2. Set the slope to zero.\(2(x-a)+2(x-b)=0\)
  3. Solve.\(2x = a+b \;\Rightarrow\; x=\dfrac{a+b}{2}\)
  4. Check it's a minimum.\(C''(x)=4>0\) — a valley (more on \(C''\) in Week 3).
✓
\(x^\star=\frac{a+b}{2}\): the midpoint. With \(n\) houses you'd get \(x^\star=\frac{1}{n}\sum a_i\) — the mean. Minimizing squared error ⇒ averaging.
🪙 Bonus connection: maximum likelihood

The "coin toss" segment optimizes a different cost. If you flip a coin and see \(h\) heads in \(n\) tosses, the probability \(p\) that makes the data most likely is found by setting the derivative of the (log-)likelihood to zero — giving \(p^\star=\tfrac{h}{n}\), the observed frequency. Same machinery, different objective. Taking \(\log\) turns nasty products into friendly sums before differentiating.

Week 2

Gradients & Gradient Descent

Many variables · the workhorse of ML

Real models have thousands or millions of parameters, not one. The gradient packages all their slopes into a single vector that points uphill — and walking the other way, gradient descent, is how almost every model on Earth is trained.

Functions of several variables

A function like \(f(x,y)\) takes two inputs and returns one number — picture a landscape: \((x,y)\) is your position and \(f\) is the altitude. The cost of a 2-parameter model \((m,b)\) is exactly such a surface, and we want its lowest point.

As a surface

As a contour map

🗺️ Two views, one function

The bowl \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2\) is a paraboloid (left) whose contour lines — sets of equal height — are circles (right). Gradient descent is easiest to follow on the contour map: you always step perpendicular to the contours, straight downhill.

Partial derivatives

A partial derivative asks: if I nudge just one input and freeze the rest, how does \(f\) change? Notation \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\) or \(f_x\).

⭐ The only rule you need

To compute \(\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\), treat every other variable as a constant and differentiate normally with respect to \(x\). That's it.

Example\(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2\)
  1. \(\partial/\partial x\): treat \(y\) as constant.\(y^2\) is a constant ⇒ derivative \(0\). So \(f_x = 2x\).
  2. \(\partial/\partial y\): treat \(x\) as constant.\(x^2\) is a constant ⇒ \(f_y = 2y\).
✓
\(f_x = 2x,\quad f_y = 2y\).
Example\(f(x,y)=3x^2y^3\)
  1. \(f_x\): hold \(y^3\) fixed.\(\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3y^3\cdot x^2)=3y^3\cdot 2x = 6xy^3\)
  2. \(f_y\): hold \(3x^2\) fixed.\(\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2\cdot y^3)=3x^2\cdot 3y^2 = 9x^2y^2\)
✓
\(f_x = 6xy^3,\quad f_y = 9x^2y^2\).

The gradient: a vector of all the slopes

Stack the partial derivatives into a vector. That's the gradient \(\nabla f\) (read "del f"):

$$\nabla f(x,y)=\begin{pmatrix}\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}\\[6pt]\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}\end{pmatrix}\qquad\text{e.g. for } x^2+y^2:\quad \nabla f=\begin{pmatrix}2x\\2y\end{pmatrix}$$
⭐ What the gradient means

The gradient points in the direction of steepest ascent, and its length is how steep that is. So \(-\nabla f\) points straight downhill — the fastest way to decrease the function.

🎯 Optimization in 2D

At a minimum the surface is flat in every direction, so every partial is zero: \(\nabla f = \mathbf{0}\). For \(x^2+y^2\) that means \((2x,2y)=(0,0)\Rightarrow (x,y)=(0,0)\). ✓

Gradient descent

Usually you can't solve \(\nabla f=\mathbf 0\) by hand — there are too many variables. So you walk downhill in small steps. Each step moves against the gradient, scaled by a learning rate \(\alpha\):

$$\mathbf{p}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{p}_{\text{old}} - \alpha\,\nabla f(\mathbf{p}_{\text{old}})$$

"new point = old point − (learning rate) × (gradient)"

The learning rate \(\alpha\) is the single most important knob in training. Same surface, three step sizes.
α too small

Tiny steps — it crawls and may not arrive in time.

α just right

Smooth, quick convergence into the valley.

α too large

It overshoots, bounces across the valley, or diverges.

Worked exampleOne descent step on \(f(x,y)=x^2+y^2\), start \((0.5,\,0.6)\), \(\alpha=0.1\)
  1. Gradient.\(\nabla f = (2x,2y) = (1.0,\,1.2)\) at \((0.5,0.6)\).
  2. Step against it.\((x,y) \leftarrow (0.5,0.6) - 0.1\,(1.0,1.2)\)
  3. Compute.\((0.5-0.10,\; 0.6-0.12) = (0.40,\,0.48)\)
  4. Cost dropped.\(f:\,0.61 \to 0.39\). Repeat and it homes in on \((0,0)\).
✓
New point \((0.40,\,0.48)\). Each iteration multiplies the distance to the minimum by \((1-2\alpha)\) here — geometric decay toward the bottom.

Putting it together: linear regression the payoff

Back to the house prices. We fit \(\hat y = mx + b\) by choosing \(m,b\) to minimise the squared-error cost:

$$E(m,b)=\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}\big(mx_i + b - y_i\big)^2$$

This is a bowl in \((m,b)\)-space. Its gradient (chain rule on each squared term):

$$\frac{\partial E}{\partial m}=\frac{2}{n}\sum_i (mx_i+b-y_i)\,x_i,\qquad \frac{\partial E}{\partial b}=\frac{2}{n}\sum_i (mx_i+b-y_i)$$

Method 1 — Analytical (set \(\nabla E=\mathbf 0\))

Solve the two equations \(E_m=0,\,E_b=0\) exactly. They're linear in \(m,b\), giving the famous closed form:

$$m=\frac{\overline{xy}-\bar x\,\bar y}{\overline{x^2}-\bar x^2},\quad b=\bar y - m\,\bar x$$

Exact and one-shot — but needs a matrix inverse that's too costly when there are millions of features.

Method 2 — Gradient descent

Start anywhere and repeat:

$$\begin{aligned}m &\leftarrow m - \alpha\,E_m\\ b &\leftarrow b - \alpha\,E_b\end{aligned}$$

Scales to enormous models — the same loop that trains deep nets. This is why we built all the machinery above.

Worked exampleFit a line through \((1,2),(2,2),(3,4)\) analytically
  1. Means.\(\bar x = 2,\ \bar y = \tfrac{2+2+4}{3}=\tfrac83\approx 2.667\).
  2. Needed averages.\(\overline{xy}=\tfrac{1\cdot2+2\cdot2+3\cdot4}{3}=\tfrac{18}{3}=6,\quad \overline{x^2}=\tfrac{1+4+9}{3}=\tfrac{14}{3}\approx4.667\).
  3. Slope.\(m=\dfrac{\overline{xy}-\bar x\bar y}{\overline{x^2}-\bar x^2}=\dfrac{6-2(2.667)}{4.667-4}=\dfrac{0.667}{0.667}=1\).
  4. Intercept.\(b=\bar y-m\bar x = 2.667-1(2)=0.667\).
✓
Best-fit line: \(\hat y = x + \tfrac23\). Gradient descent, run long enough from any start, lands on this same \((m,b)\).

Week 3

Optimization in Neural Networks

Second derivatives · Newton · backprop

A zero slope alone can't tell a peak from a valley from a saddle. The second derivative measures curvature and settles the question — it powers Newton's method, the Hessian test, and ultimately the backpropagation that trains neural networks.

The second derivative: curvature

Differentiate twice. The second derivative \(f''(x)=\dfrac{d^2f}{dx^2}\) is the rate of change of the rate of change — how fast the slope itself is turning.

🚗 Position → velocity → acceleration

If \(h(t)=-5t^2+20t\) is a ball's height, then velocity is \(h'(t)=-10t+20\) and acceleration is \(h''(t)=-10\) (constant gravity). First derivative = speed; second derivative = how the speed changes.

Sign of \(f''\)ShapeLooks likeA critical point (\(f'=0\)) is a…
\(f''>0\)Concave up ⌣a cup / valleyminimum
\(f''<0\)Concave down ⌢a cap / hillmaximum
\(f''=0\)inflection / flatstraightening outinconclusive
Curvature decides the type of a flat point. Same idea, opposite signs.
⭐ The second-derivative test (1D)

Find where \(f'(x)=0\). Then check \(f''\) there: positive ⇒ minimum, negative ⇒ maximum. This is how you prove a critical point is the bottom of a valley.

Newton's method — a smarter way downhill

Gradient descent only knows the slope, so it inches along. Newton's method also uses curvature (the second derivative) to jump much closer to the target in one bound.

First, root-finding

To solve \(f(x)=0\): stand at \(x_k\), slide down the tangent line until it hits the x-axis, and land at:

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\frac{f(x_k)}{f'(x_k)}$$

Then, optimization

To minimize \(g(x)\) we want \(g'(x)=0\) — that's a root-finding problem for \(g'\). Apply Newton to \(g'\) instead of \(g\), which brings in the second derivative:

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\frac{g'(x_k)}{g''(x_k)}$$
Worked exampleMinimize \(g(x)=e^{x}-\ln x\) with Newton's method
  1. Derivatives.\(g'(x)=e^{x}-\dfrac1x,\qquad g''(x)=e^{x}+\dfrac1{x^2}\).
  2. We need \(g'(x)=0\), i.e. \(e^{x}=\dfrac1x\).No clean algebraic solution — so iterate.
  3. Start at \(x_0=1\).\(g'(1)=e-1=1.718,\ g''(1)=e+1=3.718\).
    \(x_1 = 1-\dfrac{1.718}{3.718}=0.538\).
  4. Iterate again.\(x_2 = 0.538-\dfrac{g'(0.538)}{g''(0.538)}=0.566\), then \(x_3=0.5671\).
✓
Minimum at \(x^\star \approx 0.5671\) — reached in ~3 steps. \(g''>0\) everywhere here, confirming it's a valley. Newton's quadratic convergence roughly doubles the correct digits each step.
👍 Newton's upside

Very fast near the answer; uses curvature, so it adapts its step size automatically — no learning rate to tune.

👎 The catch for ML

It needs the Hessian (all second derivatives) and its inverse. With millions of parameters that matrix is gigantic, so deep learning sticks with gradient descent and its cheaper cousins.

The Hessian & saddle points

In many variables, the "second derivative" becomes a matrix of all second partials — the Hessian \(H\):

$$H=\begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy}\\ f_{yx} & f_{yy}\end{pmatrix}\qquad(f_{xy}=f_{yx}\text{ for smooth }f)$$
⭐ The Hessian test (2 variables)

At a critical point (where \(\nabla f=\mathbf 0\)), compute \(\det H = f_{xx}f_{yy}-f_{xy}^2\):

  • \(\det H>0\) and \(f_{xx}>0\) ⇒ local minimum (bowl up)
  • \(\det H>0\) and \(f_{xx}<0\) ⇒ local maximum (bowl down)
  • \(\det H<0\) ⇒ saddle point (up one way, down another)
  • \(\det H=0\) ⇒ test is inconclusive

Equivalently via eigenvalues of \(H\) (from \(\det(H-\lambda I)=0\)): all positive ⇒ min, all negative ⇒ max, mixed signs ⇒ saddle.

A saddle point: \(f'=0\) and \(\nabla f=\mathbf 0\), yet it's neither a max nor a min — it curves up in one direction and down in another (\(\det H<0\)).
Example · minimum\(f=2x^2+3y^2-xy\)
  1. First partials → critical point.\(f_x=4x-y,\ f_y=6y-x\Rightarrow (0,0)\).
  2. Second partials.\(f_{xx}=4,\ f_{yy}=6,\ f_{xy}=-1\).
  3. Hessian & determinant.\(H=\begin{psmallmatrix}4&-1\\-1&6\end{psmallmatrix},\ \det H = 24-1=23>0\).
  4. Classify.\(\det H>0\) and \(f_{xx}=4>0\).
✓
Local minimum at \((0,0)\). Eigenvalues \(5\pm\sqrt2\) — both positive. ✓
Example · saddle\(f=2x^2-2y^2\)
  1. Critical point.\(f_x=4x,\ f_y=-4y\Rightarrow (0,0)\).
  2. Second partials.\(f_{xx}=4,\ f_{yy}=-4,\ f_{xy}=0\).
  3. Determinant.\(\det H = (4)(-4)-0=-16<0\).
✓
Saddle point — a minimum along \(x\), a maximum along \(y\). Eigenvalues \(+4,-4\) (mixed signs). These are everywhere in deep-learning loss landscapes.

Neural networks: a stack of functions ML

A neural network is just nested functions. Each layer takes the previous layer's output \(a^{[l-1]}\), applies a linear map (weights \(w\), bias \(b\)), then a nonlinear activation \(\sigma\):

$$z^{[l]}=w^{[l]}a^{[l-1]}+b^{[l]},\qquad a^{[l]}=\sigma\!\big(z^{[l]}\big)$$

Running these layer by layer from input \(x\) to prediction \(\hat y\) is the forward pass:

Forward pass: data flows left → right, each layer applying "linear map, then activation." Backprop later sends gradients right → left.

The most common activation in this course is the sigmoid, whose derivative folds back on itself — a gift for backprop:

$$\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}},\qquad \sigma'(z)=\sigma(z)\big(1-\sigma(z)\big)=a(1-a)$$

We score the prediction with a loss \(L(y,\hat y)\) (e.g. squared error \(\tfrac12(\hat y-y)^2\) or log-loss). Training = minimise \(L\) over all the weights and biases by gradient descent. The only missing piece: how to get \(\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) for weights buried deep in the stack. That's backpropagation.

Backpropagation = the chain rule, run backward

To update a weight we need its effect on the loss. The weight sits at the bottom of a chain \(w \to z \to a \to L\), so by the chain rule we multiply the local derivatives along that chain — computing them from the output backward to reuse work.

Worked exampleBackprop through one sigmoid neuron: \(\;a=\sigma(z),\ z=wx+b,\ L=\tfrac12(a-y)^2\)
  1. Local derivative — loss vs activation.\(\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial a}=(a-y)\)
  2. Activation vs pre-activation (sigmoid).\(\dfrac{\partial a}{\partial z}=a(1-a)\)
  3. Pre-activation vs weight.\(\dfrac{\partial z}{\partial w}=x\)
  4. Multiply the chain.\(\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w}=\underbrace{(a-y)}_{\partial L/\partial a}\cdot\underbrace{a(1-a)}_{\partial a/\partial z}\cdot\underbrace{x}_{\partial z/\partial w}\)
  5. Update the weight.\(w \leftarrow w-\alpha\,\dfrac{\partial L}{\partial w}\) (and similarly \(b\leftarrow b-\alpha(a-y)a(1-a)\)).
✓
The whole of deep-learning training is this chain, automated across millions of weights. The chain rule from Week 1 is the engine of backpropagation.

Gradients & descent

$$\nabla f=\Big(\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\dots,\tfrac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\Big)$$ $$\mathbf p \leftarrow \mathbf p-\alpha\,\nabla f$$

Minimum ⟺ \(\nabla f=\mathbf0\). \(-\nabla f\) is steepest descent; \(\alpha\) is the learning rate.

Curvature: Newton & Hessian

$$x_{k+1}=x_k-\tfrac{g'(x_k)}{g''(x_k)}$$ $$H=\begin{psmallmatrix}f_{xx}&f_{xy}\\f_{yx}&f_{yy}\end{psmallmatrix}$$

\(\det H>0,f_{xx}>0\Rightarrow\) min; \(\det H>0,f_{xx}<0\Rightarrow\) max; \(\det H<0\Rightarrow\) saddle.

🤖 The one-line summary of the whole course

Training a model = minimising a cost. Differentiate the cost, set the gradient to zero (or walk against it), and use the second derivative to know you've found a valley. Backpropagation is just the chain rule applying this to every weight in a neural network.

Glossary

Key terms, plain English
Derivative \(f'\)
Instantaneous rate of change; the slope of the tangent line. Limit of rise-over-run as the run shrinks to zero.
Tangent line
The straight line that just grazes a curve at a point, matching its slope there.
Power rule
\(\frac{d}{dx}x^n=nx^{n-1}\) — bring the exponent down, subtract one.
Chain rule
Derivative of a composition: outside′ × inside′. The mathematical engine of backpropagation.
\(e\)
≈ 2.71828, the limit of \((1+1/n)^n\); \(e^x\) is its own derivative.
Partial derivative
Derivative with respect to one variable, holding the others constant.
Gradient \(\nabla f\)
Vector of all partial derivatives; points in the direction of steepest ascent.
Gradient descent
Iterative optimization: repeatedly step against the gradient by a learning rate \(\alpha\).
Learning rate \(\alpha\)
Step-size knob. Too small = slow; too large = overshoot/diverge.
Cost / loss
A number measuring how wrong a model is; training minimises it.
Least squares
Minimising the sum of squared errors; its minimiser is the mean / best-fit line.
Second derivative \(f''\)
Rate of change of the slope; measures curvature (concavity).
Concave up / down
\(f''>0\): cup-shaped (valley). \(f''<0\): cap-shaped (hill).
Newton's method
Optimization using curvature: \(x_{k+1}=x_k-g'/g''\). Converges quadratically.
Hessian \(H\)
Matrix of all second partial derivatives; its sign-structure classifies critical points.
Saddle point
A flat point that's a min in one direction and a max in another (\(\det H<0\)).
Sigmoid \(\sigma\)
\(1/(1+e^{-z})\); squashes any number into \((0,1)\). Derivative \(=a(1-a)\).
Forward pass
Computing a network's prediction by running inputs through every layer.
Backpropagation
Computing loss gradients for every weight by applying the chain rule backward through the network.
